1. Introduction

Current LLMs Capabilities: Large language models (LLMs) are capable of understanding linguistic context and generating coherent text.

Inherent Limitations:


Outdated Information: LLMs are trained on data up to a specific cutoff time and lack knowledge of subsequent events.


Private & Noisy Data: Challenges include handling private data, noisy information, and limited control over sources, leading to reduced trustworthiness.


Hallucination: Models may generate incorrect or unsupported answers when internal or external knowledge is missing.


Lack of Domain-Specific Knowledge: Insufficient information in specialized fields like law and medicine.


Solution (RAG): Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) leverages external data sources to improve LLM effectiveness.


Retrieval Process: Searching for information from external sources that match the input query.


Benefits: Accessed knowledge is more up-to-date and accurate.


Framework: Combines retrieval (finding data) and generation (integrating data with the query).

2. Architecture of RAG
2.1 Retriever
The retriever acts as an information provider, returning relevant documents by measuring similarity with the input query.

2.1.1 Sparse Retrieval
Relies on matching terms using an inverted index.


Mechanism: External data is processed into an indexed representation of individual words.

Algorithms:

TF-IDF:


Term Frequency (TF): Measures how often a term appears in a chunk; more frequency implies more importance.


Inverse Document Frequency (IDF): Reduces importance of common terms (stopwords) and increases it for rare ones.


Limitations: Eliminates terms appearing in all chunks; ignores morphological variations; lacks semantic understanding or synonym recognition.


BM25: An improved version of TF-IDF.


Properties: Features term frequency saturation and document length normalization (shorter documents are prioritized for the same term frequency).


Limitations: Cannot capture synonyms and performs poorly on long, complex queries.


General Advantages: Fast retrieval, no training required, efficient storage.


General Disadvantages: Lacks semantic/contextual understanding and is sensitive to query/database quality.

2.1.2 Dense Retrieval
Encodes queries and documents into high-dimensional vectors and computes similarity (e.g., dot product or cosine similarity).

Architectures:


Generator as Embedding Model: Uses the generation model for retrieval to align vector spaces, but is computationally expensive and potentially less accurate than specialized models.


Bi-encoder: Uses two separate encoders (e.g., BERT) for queries and documents.


Pros: Faster inference (embeddings can be precomputed) and better scalability.


Cons: Representations may not fully align; requires additional training.


Summary: Captures semantic meaning and handles synonyms/context. Challenges include high computational/memory costs and lower performance on exact keyword matching.

2.1.3 Retrieval Granularity
Refers to the unit of indexing.


Passage-level: Faster than document-level but may contain redundant info.


Proposition-level: Uses atomic statements; precise but requires expensive LLM-based preprocessing.


Token-level: Individual tokens; fast and handles rare terms but requires massive storage.


Adaptive Granularity: Uses hierarchical indices (e.g., Sentence Window Retrieval or Auto-merging Retrieval).

2.2 Generation
Integrates retrieved data to improve accuracy.

Architectures:

Encoder–Decoder: Separates encoding and sequential decoding (e.g., machine translation). Good for semantics but complex to scale.

Decoder-only: Treats input/output as one sequence using masked self-attention. Effective for long-form text and reasoning.


Accessibility: Categorized into White-box (parameter-accessible for fine-tuning) and Black-box (inaccessible parameters like GPT or Gemini).

2.3 Augmentation
Connects the retriever and generator.

Input-Layer Integration: Merges info directly into the query. Constrained by context window size (mitigated by truncation or Fusion-in-Decoder).

Output-Layer Integration:


kNN-LM: Combines LM and retrieval predictions at every token step; high accuracy but slow and may reduce fluency.


REFEED: Refines an initial response using retrieved data; better reasoning but higher latency.

Intermediate-Layer Integration: Injects representations into hidden layers. Enables deep interaction but requires internal model access and further training.

3. Training Approaches
3.1 Training-free Approaches
Incorporates data into prompts without updating parameters. Resource-efficient for large models.

Prompt Engineering-based:


In-Context RALM: Simple appending of data.


IRCoT: Interleaves generation and retrieval for multi-step reasoning.


GENREAD: Generates contextual documents before answering.


SKR: Model decides if retrieval is necessary.


ToC: Handles ambiguous queries via sub-questions.


Retrieval-Guided Token Generation: Grounding at the token level (e.g., kNN-LM) to reduce hallucination, though slow.

3.2 Independent & Sequential Training
Independent: Retriever and LLM are trained separately. Flexible and low cost, but representations may be misaligned.


Sequential: One component is trained and fixed before the other.


Retriever-first (e.g., RETRO): Uses fixed embeddings; stable but dependent on retrieval quality.


LLM-first (e.g., DKRR, AAR): Retriever learns from LLM supervision; captures logical relevance but is computationally expensive.

Joint Training (e.g., REALM): Simultaneous optimization. Reduces error propagation but is highly complex and costly.

4. Applications & 5. Challenges

Applications: Question Answering, Fact Verification, Recommendation Systems, and Domain-specific tasks.


Challenges: Privacy risks, limited modality (mostly text-focused), and increased latency due to the retrieval step.
