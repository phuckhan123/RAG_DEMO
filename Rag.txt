1. Introduction 
Current large language models (LLMs) are capable of understanding linguistic context and 
generating coherent text; however, they still face several inherent limitations. For example: - 
Outdated internal information: LLMs are trained on data up to a specific cutoff time. 
Therefore, they lack knowledge of events occurring afterward. Although recent AI systems 
incorporate web browsing capabilities, they still encounter challenges such as handling private 
data, the presence of noisy information, and limited control over data sources, which leads to 
reduced trustworthiness.  - 
Hallucination: When a query is not covered by the internal knowledge or cannot be 
retrieved from external sources, the model may generate answers that are incorrect or 
unsupported by factual evidence.  - 
Lack of domain-specific knowledge: LLMs may lack sufficient information in specialized 
domains such as law and medicine.  
→ To address these limitations, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is employed to 
leverage external data sources instead of relying solely on internal knowledge, thereby improving 
the effectiveness of LLMs. 
Retrieval is the process of searching for relevant information from external data sources that 
match the input query. Since it accesses external knowledge, the retrieved information can be 
more up-to-date and accurate. 
RAG is a framework that combines retrieval and generation. It first retrieves relevant 
information from external data sources, then integrates this information with the input query to 
improve the quality of the generated output. 
2. Architecture of RAG 
2.1 Retriever 
The retriever acts as an information provider in RAG systems. It returns information relevant to 
the input query by measuring the similarity between the query and documents in external data 
sources. 
Based on the retrieval mechanism, retrievers can be categorized into two types: sparse retrieval 
and dense retrieval. 
2.1.1 Sparse Retrieval

from raw input data. 
External data is processed into an indexed representation, where indices are associated with 
individual words. The system then matches these indexed terms with the input query to retrieve 
relevant data segments. Two representative algorithms used in sparse retrieval are TF-IDF and 
BM25.

2.1.2 Dense Retrieval 
Dense retrieval methods encode both queries and documents into high-dimensional vector 
representations, and then compute similarity between the query vector and document vectors. 
A commonly used similarity function is the dot product (or cosine similarity):

A straightforward design for dense retrieval is to reuse the generation model as an embedding 
model for retrieval. In this approach, both the retriever and the generator represent queries and 
documents within the same vector space, which improves alignment between retrieval and 
generation. As a result, the retriever can identify documents that are more relevant from the 
perspective of the generator. 
However, since this approach does not use a specialized embedding model such as BERT, it may 
be less effective in accurately measuring similarity. In addition, using the generation model for 
both retrieval and generation increases computational cost and resource consumption. 
Another widely used architecture is the bi-encoder. This approach employs two separate 
encoders (typically based on BERT) to independently encode queries and documents.One 
encoder processes the query. The other encoder processes the documents  
Advantages: - - - 
Faster inference, since document embeddings can be precomputed and stored  
Better scalability, as encoding documents separately is more efficient than repeatedly 
processing them with a generation model  
Reduced computational cost during retrieval  
Disadvantages: - - 
Since queries and documents are encoded independently, the representations may 
not fully align  
Additional training is required to improve interaction between the retriever and the 
generator  
Summary of Dense Retrieval 
In contrast to sparse retrieval, dense retrieval captures the semantic meaning of both queries and 
documents, rather than relying solely on exact term matching. Therefore, it can: Handle 
synonymous expressions. Capture contextual meaning. Be trained and optimized for specific tasks  
However, dense retrieval also faces several challenges: High computational cost for embedding 
generation. Significant memory requirements for storing high-dimensional vectors. Lower 
performance on exact keyword matching tasks (e.g., retrieving a specific token such as “ABC”). 
2.1.3 Retrieval Granularity 
Retrieval granularity refers to the unit of retrieval and indexing used in the corpus. Common 
granularity levels include: Passage-level, Proposition-level,Token-level. 
Passage-level Retrieval 
Retrieval is performed at the level of text passages (paragraphs or chunks). This Granularity  
Faster than document-level retrieval and more efficient in terms of memory usage. But retrieved 
passages may still contain irrelevant or redundant information. 
Proposition-level Retrieval 
Retrieval is performed at the level of propositions (atomic statements). Advantages: More concise 
and precise and reduced noise in retrieved results. Disadvantages: requires expensive 
preprocessing and often relies on LLMs to extract propositions from raw text. 
Token-level Retrieval 
Retrieval operates at the level of individual tokens. So this have very fast retrieval and effective for 
handling rare or specific terms Although requires significantly larger storage space. 
Adaptive Granularity 
In addition to fixed granularity, there exists an adaptive granularity approach. 
Instead of retrieving from a single index, the system constructs hierarchical indices (e.g., 
document → passage → sentence → proposition). 
Several techniques are used in this setting: - - 
Sentence Window Retrieval: after retrieving a relevant sentence, surrounding 
sentences are included to provide additional context.  
Auto-merging Retrieval: if multiple related sentences are retrieved, they are merged 
into a larger coherent context. 
2.2 Generation 
Generation in RAG differs from normal generation in that it integrates retriggered data to improve 
the accuracy of the generated content. The generation component in RAG systems can be broadly 
categorized into two main architectures: encoder–decoder and decoder-only models. 
Encoder–Decoder Architecture 
This architecture separates the model into two components: - 
Encoder: encodes the input query into a high-dimensional vector representation  
- 
Decoder: generates output tokens sequentially based on the encoded representation 
using a cross-attention mechanism, while consuming previously generated tokens as 
additional input  
Advantages: - - 
Captures the structure and semantics of the input effectively  
Well-suited for tasks such as machine translation  
Disadvantages: - - 
More complex architecture  
More difficult to scale compared to decoder-only models 
Decoder-only Architecture. In this architecture, both input and output are treated as a single 
sequence. The model uses masked self-attention, where each generated token can only attend to 
previous tokens and not future ones. So this effective for long-form text generation and strong 
performance in reasoning tasks.  
Generator Accessibility. Generation models can also be categorized based on parameter 
accessibility: Parameter-accessible (white-box) models and parameter-inaccessible (black-box) 
models. 
2.2.1 Parameter-Accessible Generators (White-box) 
These generators allow parameter optimization and fine-tuning, enabling better adaptation to 
retrieval and augmentation mechanisms. 
2.2.2 Parameter-Inaccessible Generators (Black-box) 
These include LLMs where internal parameters are not accessible or modifiable (e.g., GPT, 
Gemini). Even for models that support fine-tuning, their large scale makes adaptation difficult, 
especially in low-resource domains. 
Therefore, a practical approach is to optimize the retrieval and augmentation components instead 
of modifying the generator. 
2.3 Augmentation 
Augmentation is the process that connects the retriever and the generator, enabling the generator to 
utilize retrieved information. This is a critical component in Retrieval-Augmented LLMs (RA
LLMs) and is commonly categorized into three types: 
2.3.1 Input-Layer Integration 
In this approach, retrieved information is directly integrated into the original query and passed to 
the generator. 
Although effective, this method is constrained by the length of retrieved information, as the input 
may exceed the model’s context window. 
To mitigate this limitation: - - 
RAG-LLM variants may truncate tokens from the beginning of the input  
Fusion-in-Decoder (FiD) processes each retrieved document independently within the 
encoder  
This approach is commonly used in black-box generation-based RAG, where internal parameters 
are inaccessible. 
Advantages: - - - 
High flexibility  
Easy to implement  
Compatible with different LLMs  
Disadvantages: - - 
Limited by context window size  
Potential information loss when retrieved content is too long  
2.3.2 Output-Layer Integration 
This approach combines retrieval results with generated outputs. 
kNN-LM 
At each token generation step, predictions are made from both the language model and retrieved 
information. These predictions are combined to select the final token. Characteristics: 
High accuracy due to token-level integration . Slow generation speed due to per-token 
computation need look both retriever and generation. May reduce fluency compared to standard 
LLM outputs 
REFEED 
The model first generates an initial response, then revisits the retrieved data to refine and improve 
the answer. So this have better reasoning compared to kNN-LM, since retrieval is used after initial 
generation. But requires multiple passes through the LLM therefore higher computational cost and 
latency.  
2.3.3 Intermediate-Layer Integration 
This approach integrates retrieved information directly into the internal layers of the generation 
model, rather than appending it to the input. Specifically, retrieved representations are injected into 
the model’s hidden layers, enabling deeper interaction between the retrieved content and the 
generation process. Such integration can enhance the model’s capability by improving its 
understanding of retrieved information and alleviating constraints imposed by the context window, 
making it suitable for iterative or repeated retrieval scenarios. However, this approach requires 
deep access to the model’s internal architecture, which is often not feasible for modern large-scale 
LLMs. Additionally, it necessitates further training to enable effective interaction with retrieved 
data, resulting in increased computational and implementation costs. 
3. Training 
Based on whether training is required, RAG approaches can be broadly categorized into two types: 
training-free approaches and training-based approaches. 
3.1 Training-free Approaches 
Training-free methods directly incorporate retrieved data into the prompt without updating model 
parameters. Since modern LLMs contain a very large number of parameters, fine-tuning and 
updating stored knowledge become computationally expensive and time-consuming. Therefore, 
training-free approaches are more resource-efficient but may be limited in optimizing how 
retrieved data is utilized by the generator. Based on how retrieved information is used, these 
approaches can be further divided into:Prompt Engineering-based Methods and Retrieval-Guided 
Token Generation Methods. 
3.1.1 Prompt Engineering-based Methods 
Since LLMs are highly dependent on input queries, these methods incorporate retrieved data as 
contextual information into the original prompt to guide generation. 
Examples include: - - - 
In-Context RALM: directly appends retrieved data to the prompt. This approach is 
simple and easy to implement but is constrained by the context window when 
documents are long.  
IRCoT: interleaves generation and retrieval processes, allowing the system to retrieve 
more relevant information for subsequent reasoning steps compared to standard single
query retrieval methods. This makes it suitable for complex multi-step reasoning tasks. 
However, it is computationally expensive and introduces latency due to repeated calls 
to both the retriever and the LLM.  
GENREAD: first prompts an LLM to generate contextual documents for a query, and 
then uses these generated documents to produce the final answer.  
- - 
SKR: guides the LLM to determine whether it can answer a query independently and 
only performs retrieval when necessary. However, it is challenging to train the model 
to decide when retrieval is required, which may lead to incorrect responses.  
ToC (Tree-of-Clarifications): retrieves documents related to ambiguous queries, 
constructs a recursive structure of disambiguated sub-questions, and aggregates them to 
generate the final answer. This method is effective for handling ambiguous queries but 
introduces higher latency and a more complex workflow. 
3.1.2 Retrieval-Guided Token Generation Methods 
These methods incorporate retrieved information at the token generation level. For example, kNN
LM (discussed earlier) uses retrieval information at each token generation step. This reduces 
hallucination by grounding generation in external data. However, because retrieval is performed at 
each step, these methods are computationally expensive and result in slower generation. 
3.2 Independent Training 
Independent training refers to training the retriever and the LLM separately without interaction. 
During training: - - 
The retriever is optimized to improve retrieval quality  
The LLM is optimized to better utilize retrieved data  
A common training objective for LLMs is the negative log-likelihood (NLL): 
ℒ =−log⁡PLLM(y ∣ x) 
Where: 
−⁡x: input query or context  
−⁡y: target output (ground truth)  
−⁡PLLM(y ∣ x): probability assigned by the model  
The goal is to maximize P(y ∣ x)using backpropagation by minimizing the loss. 
For the retriever, training methods depend on whether it is sparse or dense. 
Independent training offers lower computational cost and a simpler training process, while 
maintaining high flexibility, as external data can be updated without requiring retraining of the 
entire system. However, since the retriever and generator are optimized separately rather than 
jointly, their representations may not be well aligned, which can limit the overall reasoning 
performance of the system. 
3.2 Sequential Training 
To improve alignment between retrieval and generation, sequential training is used. In this 
approach, one component is trained first and then fixed, while the other component is trained 
afterward. Based on training order, sequential training can be divided into: Retriever-first and 
LLM-first.  
3.3.1 Retriever-first 
The retriever is trained first and then fixed. The LLM is subsequently trained to better utilize 
retrieved data. 
Examples include: - - 
RETRO: uses a pre-trained BERT-based retriever and integrates retrieved chunks into 
an encoder–decoder architecture  
RALMs: use external retrievers such as Google Search or ColBERTv2, followed by 
fine-tuning the LLM 
This approach leverages existing pre-trained models (e.g., BERT, Cohere), reducing the need for 
training from scratch and thereby lowering overall training cost. In addition, the use of a fixed 
embedding model ensures a stable vector space, which contributes to more consistent retrieval 
performance. However, the approach is highly dependent on retrieval quality, and inaccuracies in 
the retrieved information can directly propagate to and negatively affect the quality of the 
generated output. 
3.3.2 LLM-first 
This approach treats the LLM as the central component and trains the retriever under its 
supervision. Examples include: - - 
DKRR: uses attention scores from sequence-to-sequence models to generate 
supervision signals for retriever training  
AAR: uses a smaller language model to generate supervision signals, making it suitable 
for black-box LLMs  
Advantages: - 
Retriever learns to capture logical relevance aligned with LLM reasoning  
Limitations: - - 
High computational cost due to repeated LLM inference  
Errors in LLM reasoning may propagate to retriever training  
3.3.3 Joint Training 
Joint training optimizes both the retriever and the generator simultaneously. 
RAG models are trained by minimizing the log-likelihood objective over both components.For 
example, REALM uses Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS): 
arg⁡max⁡(q⊤d) 
where higher similarity between query qand document dleads to higher relevance. 
Advantages: - - 
End-to-end optimization  
Reduces error propagation  
Disadvantages: - - 
Highly complex training process  
Very high computational cost due to simultaneous parameter updates 
4. Applications 
RAG has a wide range of applications in LLM systems, including: - - - - 
Question Answering (QA): provides more accurate responses by grounding answers in 
external, reliable data sources  
Fact Verification: evaluates the correctness of information using external data  
Recommendation Systems: models user preferences and provides personalized 
recommendations. For example, Di Palma [25] proposes retrieving information from 
movies, images, and books to improve recommendation quality  
Domain-specific applications: RAG is particularly useful in specialized domains where 
standard LLMs lack sufficient knowledge. External data enables deeper understanding 
and improves generation quality 
5. Challenges 
Despite its advantages, RAG faces several challenges: - - - 
Privacy risks: incorporating external data may expose sensitive or private information  
Limited modality: current RAG systems primarily focus on textual data. Integration 
with multimodal data (e.g., images, audio) is still in early stages and requires further 
research  
Latency: since retrieval must be performed before generation, RAG systems are 
generally slower than standard LLMs
